Behavior as Communication2 of 10

Behavior is information. It tells us what a child's brain needs, even when the child cannot say it in words.

Before asking "How do I stop this behavior?", ask: "What is this behavior trying to tell me?"

"All behavior is a form of communication. The question is not how to stop it — but what it is saying."

Caregiver Skill: Pause and Look

  • Separate the behavior from the child
  • Look for patterns — time, place, before-and-after
  • Ask: Is this fatigue, sensory overload, transition stress?
  • Practice: Use Tool A1 to track one moment

Brain-Based Expectations3 of 10

Children with FASD or NDD may have developmental capacity well below their chronological age — especially in executive function, emotional regulation, and cause-effect reasoning.

Adjusting expectations is not lowering standards; it's meeting the child where they actually are.

Key Principle

  • Assume 50–70% of chronological age for executive function
  • Avoid "he should know better by now" thinking
  • Match demand to capacity, not to age

Sensory Overload and Shutdown4 of 10

Sensory processing differences are common in FASD/NDD. A child's brain may not filter or integrate sensory input the way typical brains do. What looks like a meltdown may be a sensory overload response.

Common Sensory Triggers

  • Noise — loud spaces, multiple conversations, sudden sounds
  • Touch — unexpected physical contact, clothing textures
  • Visual — bright lights, clutter, fast-moving images
  • Proprioceptive — difficulty knowing body position

Emotional Regulation and Co-Regulation5 of 10

Children with FASD/NDD often struggle to regulate their own emotions. They depend on a caregiver's calm presence to help them return to balance — this is co-regulation.

"Your calm is their anchor. Before trying to reason, help the nervous system settle."

Co-Regulation Skills

  • Lower your voice, slow your breathing
  • Offer proximity without demand
  • Use fewer words, not more
  • Model calm even when you don't feel it

Trauma, Grief, Attachment, and Trust6 of 10

Many families caring for children with FASD/NDD are foster, kinship, or adoptive families. Prenatal exposure, early adversity, and attachment disruptions may be layered onto neurodevelopmental differences.

  • Survival responses (fight/flight/freeze/fawn) may be deeply wired
  • Trust-building takes time and consistency
  • Grief is real — for the child and the caregiver
  • Separate trauma response from behavioral choice

School vs. Home Behavior7 of 10

Many children with FASD/NDD behave very differently at school than at home. This is not manipulation — it reflects different environments, demands, and supports.

  • School: structured, predictable, lower sensory load
  • Home: unstructured, emotionally complex, recovery space
  • The child who "holds it together" at school may release everything at home
  • This is information, not contradiction

De-escalation and Daily Structure8 of 10

Predictable structure reduces cognitive load. For a child with executive function challenges, knowing what comes next is not a luxury — it is a necessity.

Practical Tools

  • Visual schedules — show, don't just tell
  • Transition warnings — 5 minutes, 2 minutes, now
  • Fewer words during escalation — the thinking brain is offline
  • After escalation: reconnect before re-teaching

When to Seek Professional Help9 of 10

This toolkit supports caregiver education and connector support. It does not replace clinical evaluation or treatment. PCP / medical home remains the clinical gatekeeper.

  • Safety concerns (self-harm, aggression, elopement) → immediate professional
  • Persistent sleep, eating, or mood concerns → discuss with PCP
  • School refusal or rapid regression → consult school team + PCP
  • Caregiver burnout affecting daily function → seek support for yourself

What Support Planning Looks Like10 of 10

Support planning is not a one-time event. It evolves with the child and family. The goal is not a perfect plan — it is a sustainable one.

  • Identify what is working — protect it
  • Identify one change to try — start small
  • Identify who is on the team — and who is missing
  • Revisit every 3–6 months
