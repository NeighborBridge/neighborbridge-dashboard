⚠️ FASD Diagnostic Pathway — From Screening to Diagnostic Readiness Internal clinical-workflow guidance

FASD diagnosis is not usually completed through a single visit or one clinician alone. The pathway from initial concern to diagnostic readiness may involve multiple phases:

  1. Initial concern / referral question — triggered by developmental, behavioral, or learning concerns
  2. Clinical screening and developmental/behavioral assessment — initial outpatient evaluation within Contentnea's usual intake process
  3. Family education about FASD diagnostic components — helping families understand what data are needed and why
  4. Records gathering — prenatal exposure history, developmental records, school and behavioral records
  5. Neurocognitive / neurobehavioral testing — essential for comprehensive diagnosis; often requires outside expertise
  6. Facial-feature assessment when indicated — part of the diagnostic picture but not sufficient alone
  7. Integrated review — when sufficient data are available across all domains
  8. Care planning and family support — continuing support regardless of diagnostic completion status

Key principle: Neurocognitive/neurobehavioral data are often essential. Even when prenatal alcohol exposure and facial-feature analysis are available, inadequate neurocognitive data may prevent a comprehensive FASD diagnosis. Richard's role is best framed as early identification, initial clinical screening, family education, and diagnostic data-navigation support — not rapid court-order diagnostic completion.

Court-order boundary applies: Richard is not currently accepting court-ordered comprehensive FASD diagnostic evaluations or forensic evaluations. Any referral should go through Contentnea's usual intake process.

📖 Diagnostic Readiness Family Education Product candidate · Internal demo-stage

Why family education is needed: Families may believe FASD diagnosis can be completed through one appointment or by one clinician. In reality, the process may require multiple evidence streams — neurocognitive/neurobehavioral testing, records review, prenatal exposure documentation, and facial-feature assessment when indicated — and may depend on outside specialty providers.

Why neurocognitive testing may matter: Even when prenatal alcohol exposure and facial-feature analysis are available, inadequate neurocognitive/neurobehavioral data may prevent a comprehensive FASD diagnosis. Helping families understand this early reduces frustration and false expectations.

Why records and outside evaluations may be needed: Schools, neuropsychology services, behavioral health providers, and other agencies may hold essential diagnostic data. Richard does not control their timelines or availability.

Why timeline cannot be promised: The diagnostic-readiness pathway depends on external testing, records release, and family data gathering — not clinic appointment availability alone. Timeline promises should not be made.
