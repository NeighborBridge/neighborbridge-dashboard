Preparing for a Possible FASD Evaluation

What Families Need to Know

Internal draft Family education Diagnostic readiness Non-PHI

Purpose / 说明

This handout explains what families may need to understand when preparing for a possible FASD evaluation. It is intended as family education and diagnostic-readiness support. It does not diagnose FASD, replace clinical evaluation, guarantee access to testing, or promise completion of a comprehensive diagnostic report.

Why FASD Evaluation May Take More Than One Visit

A FASD evaluation is not always completed through a single appointment. It may require bringing together information from several sources over time.

1

Clinical evaluation and screening

A clinician may begin with a developmental and behavioral assessment, review concerns, and provide initial impressions and recommendations.

2

Prenatal alcohol exposure history

When available, documentation of prenatal alcohol exposure may help inform the diagnostic picture.

3

Developmental and school records

Early developmental history, school records, behavioral reports, and prior evaluations can provide important context.

4

Neurocognitive / neurobehavioral testing

Testing of cognitive function, attention, memory, and behavior is often essential. This may need to be done by a specialist outside the initial clinic visit.

5

Facial-feature assessment when indicated

A physical examination may include assessment of facial features associated with prenatal alcohol exposure, when clinically appropriate.

6

Integrated review and care planning

When enough information is available across these areas, the clinician may be able to offer a more complete diagnostic picture and care plan.

Why Neurocognitive Testing Matters

Even when prenatal alcohol exposure is known and facial features are assessed, neurocognitive and neurobehavioral data are often essential for a comprehensive FASD diagnosis. Without adequate testing of brain function — such as attention, memory, executive function, and learning — it may not be possible to determine the full extent of alcohol-related effects.

Helping families understand this early can reduce frustration and set realistic expectations about the diagnostic process.

What Families Can Do to Prepare

  • ✓ Gather any available prenatal history, birth records, and early developmental information.
  • ✓ Collect school records, report cards, behavioral reports, and any prior evaluations (speech, occupational therapy, mental health, etc.).
  • ✓ Write down concerns you have noticed — behavior, attention, learning, social skills, daily functioning.
  • ✓ Ask your clinician what additional testing or referrals may be needed and where those can be obtained.
  • ✓ Understand that the diagnostic process may take time and may involve multiple providers.

Important to Understand

  • • This handout is educational. It does not provide a diagnosis.
  • • One appointment may not be enough. FASD evaluation often requires multiple information sources.
  • • Outside testing may be needed. Neurocognitive testing, school records, or specialty evaluations may come from outside providers.
  • • Timeline cannot be promised. The availability of outside testing, records, and specialty appointments varies.
  • • No legal or court-related use. This handout is not intended for court, DSS, or legal proceedings.
