FASD Diagnostic Readiness / Family Education Module

Internal Market Discovery Map

1. User Groups

Primary users (caregivers of children with suspected prenatal alcohol exposure or complex neurodevelopmental concerns):

Subgroup Pain point Current solution Gap
Foster / kinship caregivers Child has complex behavioral/learning needs; DSS or school suggests evaluation; caregiver doesn't know what to expect or how to prepare Piecemeal info from providers, online searches, word-of-mouth No structured, caregiver-facing pre-evaluation guide
Biological parents Child exhibits NDD symptoms; prenatal exposure may or may not be confirmed; parent unsure how to initiate evaluation PCP referral (if PCP knows); self-directed research No language-safe guide for parents who may feel blamed or defensive about prenatal exposure
Adoptive / guardianship families Limited or no prenatal history; behavioral challenges emerging; school suggesting evaluation School team referral; developmental pediatrician No "unknown exposure" framing — most materials assume confirmed exposure

Secondary users (professionals who support primary users):

Role Need Current alternative
Pediatric PCPs Brief guidance on what to tell families, what records to request, when to refer AAP guidelines (clinical, not family-facing); wait-and-see approach
CHWs / family navigators Conversation guide they can discuss with families (not a handout to distribute) No standard FASD navigator tool exists in most settings
School nurses / counselors Understanding of what an FASD evaluation entails; how to support family through process School-based team knowledge; varies widely

Potential sponsor categories, ranked by feasibility. No prices. No outreach.

4. Existing Alternatives

Direct competitors / substitutes

  • Proof Alliance / state FASD organizations — produce family-facing FASD materials; primarily awareness-level, not pre-evaluation readiness
  • CDC FASD materials — clinical guidelines, provider-focused; limited caregiver-facing readiness content
  • CanFASD — comprehensive but academic; not localized for US care systems
  • AAP FASD toolkit — provider toolkit; not directly distributed to families
  • NOFAS — support-network oriented; not diagnostic-readiness focused

Indirect alternatives

  • Developmental pediatrician visit — families go directly, but often unprepared; no pre-visit guidance
  • School-based evaluation (IEP) — educational, not medical; doesn't lead to FASD diagnosis
  • Online caregiver forums — variable quality, no clinical framing, no boundary language
  • ASD-focused resources — families with undiagnosed FASD may follow ASD pathway; often mismatched

5. Unmet Needs

  1. No pre-evaluation readiness tool exists that systematically walks families through what an FASD evaluation requires, what records to gather, and what questions to ask.
  2. No language-safe framing for biological parents — existing FASD materials either assume confirmed exposure (which may trigger defensiveness) or avoid the topic entirely.
  3. No PCP-facing referral prep guide — PCPs lack a simple framework for initiating the conversation with families and preparing referrals.
  4. No CHW/FASD navigator conversation guide — CHWs working with complex-Neurodevelopmental families have no structured FASD-specific conversation tool.
  5. No "unknown exposure" pathway — adoptive parents, guardians, and foster families with no prenatal history are underserved by existing FASD materials.
  6. Fragmented ecosystem — families must piece together info from CDC, state orgs, provider visits, and online forums. No integrated readiness pathway exists.

6. Safe vs Unsafe Entry Points

✅ Safe entry points (low boundary risk)

  • Pediatric PCP offices (family education materials in waiting room/patient portal)
  • FQHC care coordination teams (navigator distributes guide during complex-care planning)
  • Foster parent training programs (pre-service education module)
  • CHW training curricula (FASD awareness module for navigators)
  • Proof Alliance-type nonprofits (resource distribution, not partnership)
  • School nurse offices (family-facing info about evaluation process)

❌ Unsafe entry points (require Clara review)

  • DSS-mandated parenting education
  • Court-ordered evaluation preparation
  • Adoption agency "required reading"
  • Payer/insurance prior-authorization materials
  • Contentnea-published resource (implies endorsement)
  • Any context where material implies referral pathway

7. Top 3 Product Hypotheses

Hypothesis 1: FQHC Readiness

FQHCs with complex-pediatric populations need a caregiver-facing diagnostic readiness guide. If Contentnea leadership finds this useful for their patient population, NeighborBridge has a real-use test site and co-development partner. Risk: over-reliance on one site; implying Contentnea endorsement.

Hypothesis 2: Foster Caregiver Training Channel

Foster parent training organizations lack FASD-specific pre-evaluation materials. If integrated into training curricula, this could reach many caregivers without requiring clinical-system entry. Risk: conflation with DSS/court system if not carefully framed.

Hypothesis 3: CHW Navigation Tool

CHWs and family navigators serving complex-Neurodevelopmental families have no standard FASD education tool. A conversation-guide version could be distributed through CHW training programs and care management entities. Risk: unclear buyer; CHW programs are fragmented.
