## Core Positioning

I am exploring a community-engaged, implementation-oriented, clinically anchored faculty pathway — one that bridges early identification and long-term support for FASD / NDD across primary care, family, school, and community systems, using implementation science as a guiding methodology.

---

## Problem / Gap

FASD and related neurodevelopmental conditions are significantly under-recognized in primary care. When recognized, families rarely receive timely, coordinated support. The current landscape is fragmented:

- Primary care providers lack structured screening and referral pathways.
- Families navigate disjointed systems — clinical, school, community — with no clear navigator.
- Community health workers and school personnel are underutilized as part of a coordinated care team.
- Specialty evaluation is often delayed or inaccessible, especially for publicly insured populations.

In North Carolina, where the Medicaid transition and value-based payment reforms are underway, there is both need and opportunity to build a more coherent, scalable approach.

---

## Proposed Implementation Focus

The core of the work is a real-world care pathway anchored in primary care, with the following components:

- **Primary care / FQHC anchor** — Screening, brief intervention, and referral integrated into routine well-child and developmental visits.
- **Early identification** — Systematic use of validated screening tools tied to clear follow-up protocols.
- **Family support and navigation** — Structured support for caregivers, including education, peer connection, and resource coordination.
- **School and community coordination** — Linkage between clinical findings and school-based services, with input from community health workers.
- **CHW / navigator integration** — Training and deployment of community health workers to bridge clinical and community systems.
- **Selective specialty escalation** — Clear criteria for when and how to access developmental-behavioral pediatrics, child psychiatry, or other specialty evaluation.
- **Payer / Medicaid relevance** — Pathway design that aligns with NC Medicaid value-based care priorities, making it attractive for managed care partners and FQHC reimbursement models.

Preliminary conversations with a North Carolina FQHC and a regional Medicaid managed care organization are underway.

---

## Academic Fit

This work is more than a clinical service project. It sits at the intersection of:

- **Implementation science** — Building, testing, and refining a pathway that can be disseminated across primary care settings.
- **Community psychiatry / population health** — Addressing behavioral health needs within the communities where patients live, learn, and receive primary care.
- **Developmental-behavioral health** — A clinical anchor in the early identification and longitudinal support of NDD conditions.
- **North Carolina as a real-world implementation platform** — Active Medicaid transformation, a strong FQHC infrastructure, and receptive managed care partners create an unusual opportunity for systems work with measurable population impact.

I am in the early stages of exploring where this work might find the best academic home. Several institutional configurations are under consideration:

- A **psychiatry-facing path** — Anchored in a department of psychiatry with strong implementation science ties.
- A **family medicine / population health / community health path** — Aligned with departments that value community-engaged research and population-level outcomes.
- Other configurations that could support this kind of multi-system work — still open and being explored.

The goal is to identify the institutional fit that best supports the work, not to assume a particular destination. I would value input on what institutional configurations others have seen support this kind of cross-system implementation work.

## Sustainability Logic

A durable faculty role in this space would require alignment across several dimensions:

- **Academic legitimacy** — A departmental home that values implementation research and offers a clear scholarly identity.
- **Protected implementation effort** — Sufficient non-clinical time to build, test, and refine the pathway.
- **Local funding and payer opportunities** — External support through grants, contracts, or managed care partnerships that sustain the implementation infrastructure.
- **Clinical and community anchoring** — Enough direct clinical contact to keep the work grounded in real patient and family needs.

This alignment is an aspiration rather than an accomplished model. I am working toward it and would welcome advice on how others have built similar arrangements.
