## 1. Core Claim Assessment

**Current claim**: Trust in rural Western Hunan primary care is generated through the intersection of relational ethics (Fei Xiaotong's *chaxu geju*) and care ethics (Kleinman's moral experience), operating at three nested levels — institutional, community, and individual.

**Judgment**: The core claim is sufficiently anthropological for Anthropology and Medicine, but currently too diffuse in execution. The paper tries to cover three levels equally, which weakens the interpretive depth at each level. The strongest anthropological contribution sits at the **personal and community levels** — where the ethnography is richest and the theoretical dialogue between Fei and Kleinman is genuinely generative. The institutional-level analysis reads comparatively thinner and more programmatic.

**What needs sharpening**:
- The paper needs to decide whether the institutional level is a structural backdrop or a co-equal analytic pillar. Currently it tries to be both and succeeds at neither.
- The "three-level model" is presented as the paper's novel contribution, but the real novelty is in **how relational ethics and moral experience converge in clinical encounters** — this is the ethnographic heart. The three-level framework should serve this convergence, not compete with it.

## 2. Strongest Main Line Determination

**Judgment**: **Medical anthropology** (with a strong emphasis on **care/local moral worlds**) is the paper's strongest line.

Here is the reasoning:

| Line | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Medical anthropology** | **Strongest.** The theoretical dialogue (Fei × Kleinman), the ethnographic method, and the focus on how trust is produced through embodied clinical practice all align naturally with this line. The paper is doing anthropology of medicine, not just health services research with anthropological vocabulary. |
| **Care** | **Very strong sub-line.** The ethnographic material on listening, pulse-taking, moral attention is genuinely about care ethics. Could be foregrounded more. However, "care" alone risks being read as descriptive nursing/clinical studies unless anchored in anthropological argument. |
| **Implementation** | **Weakest.** The paper frequently drifts into operational description ("village doctors manage task overload", "institutional capacity constraints") that reads more like health systems commentary than anthropological interpretation. This is the line that most needs downweighting for Anthropology and Medicine. |
| **Local moral worlds** | **Strong natural fit.** This is practically what the paper already describes — how trust takes shape within local moral worlds shaped by relational ethics. But the paper does not use "local moral worlds" as an explicit analytic frame enough. Explicitly anchoring the argument in this Kleinman concept would strengthen fit with Anthropology and Medicine. |

**Recommendation**: Frame the paper primarily as **medical anthropology of trust as moral experience in rural Chinese clinical care**, with "local moral worlds" as the bridging concept between Fei's relational ethics and Kleinman's care ethics. Downweight implementation/policy analysis substantially.

## 3. Title

**Current**: *Trust as Moral Experience: Relational Ethics and Primary Care in Rural Western Hunan*

**Assessment**: The title is already quite strong for Anthropology and Medicine. "Trust as Moral Experience" immediately signals the anthropological register. "Relational Ethics" and "Primary Care" are appropriate. The subtitle is specific but not overly narrow.

**Suggested refinement**:
- Consider replacing "Primary Care" with "Clinical Care" or "Rural Healthcare" — "primary care" is a health-systems term that slightly pulls the paper toward implementation framing. "Rural healthcare" or "clinical practice" feels more ethnographic.
- Alternatively: *Trust as Moral Experience: Relational Ethics, Care, and the Clinical Encounter in Rural Western Hunan* — this signals the paper's actual ethnographic center (the clinical encounter) without committing to a health-systems frame.

**Comment to place**: The title is well-calibrated for Anthropology and Medicine. Minor suggestion: consider whether "Primary Care" pulls toward health-systems framing vs. the ethnographic/anthropological register you want.

## 4. Abstract

**Current**: The abstract (English) is functional but under-argued. It states the paper's aim and framework descriptively but does not convey a strong interpretive finding. It reads more like a proposal summary than a conclusion.

**Specific issues**:
- "This article explores how local networks of trust mediate the translation of China's state health policies into practice" — this frames the paper as a policy translation study, which pulls away from Anthropology and Medicine. The journal expects an anthropological claim first, not a policy mediation claim.
- The abstract does not name what the paper *argues*, only what it *explores* and *draws on*.
- "Differential mode of association" is technical Fei terminology that needs unpacking for an international audience.

**Suggested reframing**: Open with a concrete anthropological claim (e.g., "Trust in rural Chinese healthcare is not a precondition for care but is *produced through* the moral practice of care itself, within local relational orders"). Then state how the paper demonstrates this through ethnographic analysis of clinical encounters in Western Hunan.

**Comment to place**: For Anthropology and Medicine, the abstract needs to state an anthropological argument, not just a research aim. The current opening ("explores how local networks mediate policy translation") frames the paper as implementation research. Consider opening with a claim about how trust is constituted through moral experience in clinical practice.

## 5. Introduction

**Current**: The introduction continues the policy-translation framing problem. It opens with "Primary healthcare systems are complex intersections of macro-level regulations, community relations, and individual care" — this sounds like a health systems paper. The global health / public health language ("disparities", "governance structures", "resource allocation") persists through the first several paragraphs.

**Strengths**: The introduction effectively establishes Fei and Kleinman as the theoretical anchors, and the research gap is well-identified — that few studies examine how health systems are "done" through daily practice within relational structures and ethical judgment.

**What needs changing**:
- The first paragraph should *lead with the anthropological puzzle*, not the health systems complexity.
- The global health framing language ("health inequalities", "governance", "resource allocation") needs to be cut or substantially compressed. For Anthropology and Medicine, the entry point is the **moral and relational dimensions of clinical practice**, not the structural determinants of health.
- The claim that "this study provides empirical contributions to global medical anthropology" could be made more specific: what exactly is the contribution? At present it is stated as a general aspiration.

**Comment to place**: For Anthropology and Medicine, consider leading with an anthropological question rather than a health-systems framing. The opening sentences currently position the paper in global health / health policy space. The global health language (governance, disparities, resource allocation) in the first two paragraphs pulls against the journal's readership expectations. The strongest framing is the Fei × Kleinman theoretical puzzle and the question of how clinical trust is morally constituted in practice.

## 6. Findings — Personal Level

**Strongest section of the paper**. The ethnographic material on the TCM clinic, the pulse-taking, the waiting room dynamics, the embodied care interactions — this is exactly what Anthropology and Medicine publishes. The theoretical dialogue between Fei and Kleinman genuinely works here, and the writing is appropriately interpretive rather than operational.

**Recommendation**: This section needs only minor tightening. The theory could be slightly more explicit about *how* Fei and Kleinman interact in practice — currently the paper states that they "converge" but could say more about the specific mechanism of convergence in clinical interaction.

**Comment to place**: This is the paper's strongest section for Anthropology and Medicine. The embodied care descriptions (pulse-taking, listening, relational attention) are precisely the kind of ethnographic material the journal values. Consider sharpening: in a single clinical moment, *how exactly* does the Fei relational structure enable the Kleinman moral witnessing?

---

## 7. Findings — Community Level

**Second strongest section**. The ethnographic vignettes (village shop in doctor's clinic, the clinic as social space, the doctor who "stayed") are rich and well-selected. The theoretical framing is solid. The section occasionally drifts into somewhat generic statements about "community networks" that could be more analytically specific.

**Recommendation**: The strongest material is the individual doctor narratives (the director who "stayed", the young doctor who felt it was "natural" to return). These individual moral biographies are more persuasive than the general statements about guanxi networks. Lean into these life-story frames.

**Comment to place**: The strongest analytic material in this section is the individual moral biographies of practitioners choosing to stay — the director's "these people are familiar" and the young doctor's "natural to return." These provide a concrete window into moral experience that general network description does not. Consider foregrounding these narratives as the section's core evidence.

## 8. Findings — Institutional Level

**Weakest section — needs the most revision**. The institutional analysis reads primarily as health systems commentary thinly covered with anthropological vocabulary. Statements about "institutional capacity constraints", "workforce turnover", "task overload", and "policy implementation gaps" are valid but feel generic — they could describe almost any rural health system anywhere.

**For Anthropology and Medicine**: The journal will expect the institutional analysis to remain in ethnographic/anthropological register. The question should be: how is institutional authority itself *morally constituted* within local relationships? Not: what are the structural barriers to implementation?

**Recommendation**: Either (a) substantially reframe the institutional section to ask an anthropological question about how institutional legitimacy is morally negotiated, or (b) compress it significantly and make it clearly subordinate to the personal/community levels. Currently it takes up too much space for its interpretive depth.

**Comment to place**: This section reads more as health systems analysis than anthropological argument. For Anthropology and Medicine, the institutional analysis needs to be reframed around a moral/interpretive question, or significantly compressed. The strongest framing would ask: how is institutional authority itself produced — or contested — through local moral experience? The current descriptions of capacity constraints and workforce turnover, while accurate, do not add an anthropological dimension that the journal's readers would find novel.

## 9. Discussion / Conclusion

**Current**: The discussion restates the three-level model as the paper's contribution but does not push the analysis further. It reads more as a summary than an interpretive conclusion.

**What is missing**:
- A clear statement of what the Fei × Kleinman dialogue *reveals* that either alone would not
- A positioning of the paper's contribution *within* the Anthropology and Medicine literature, not just "global medical anthropology"
- A reflection on how the paper's findings complicate or extend existing theory — currently it claims to "provide empirical complement" but does not specify what it complicates or challenges
- The paper ends with somewhat generic recommendations ("understanding of local culture", "respect for patient subjectivity") that sound like policy suggestions rather than anthropological conclusions

**Recommendation**: Rewrite the conclusion to:
1. State the paper's interpretive contribution clearly in 1-2 sentences (what do we now understand about trust, care, and moral experience that we did not before?)
2. Position within Anthropology and Medicine specifically — what does this paper teach the journal's readership about care ethics in non-Western clinical settings?
3. Cut the policy-adjacent language entirely or move it to a brief implications note
4. End with an interpretive observation, not a recommendation

**Comment to place**: The conclusion restates the three-level model but does not advance the anthropological argument. For Anthropology and Medicine, the conclusion should answer: what does the Fei × Kleinman dialogue reveal about trust, care, and moral experience that neither framework alone could? The current ending with generic recommendations ("understand local culture") weakens the paper's anthropological signature. Consider ending with an interpretive claim rather than a policy suggestion.

## 10. Cross-cutting Issues

### Theoretical framing
The Fei × Kleinman dialogue is the paper's strongest analytic contribution, but the paper does not push it far enough. It repeatedly states that the two frameworks "integrate" or "converge" but does not fully theorize *how* they interact — are they complementary, in tension, operating at different scales? A more specific account of their relationship would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

### References
The paper needs to engage with Anthropology and Medicine specifically. The current reference list is generic medical anthropology / social science. Adding 3-5 citations from Anthropology and Medicine would strengthen the fit signal.

### Length / density
The paper is long for a single journal article. Consider whether the institutional-level analysis could be compressed by 30-40% and the space reallocated to deepening the personal/community ethnographic analysis.
