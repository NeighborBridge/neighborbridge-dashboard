PCP Referral Preparation Guide

Talking with Your Child’s Doctor About a Possible FASD Evaluation

Internal draft PCP-facing Referral preparation Non-PHI

This guide complements the FASD Diagnostic Readiness Family Education Module. It is meant to help caregivers prepare for a conversation with their child’s pediatrician or primary care provider. It does not diagnose FASD, guarantee access to evaluation, or replace clinical judgment.

1. When to Have This Conversation

Consider raising the possibility of an FASD evaluation with your child’s doctor if your child:

  • • Has ongoing learning or attention difficulties that are not fully explained by a known diagnosis
  • • Shows behavioral patterns that are difficult to manage despite supports at home or school
  • • Has delays in adaptive skills (daily living, social judgment, safety awareness) that seem inconsistent with their overall abilities
  • • Has a history of prenatal alcohol exposure that has not yet been fully evaluated
  • • Has no confirmed prenatal history but shows a pattern of neurodevelopmental concerns that have not responded to standard interventions

2. Questions to Ask Your Child’s Doctor

You can start the conversation by saying something like:

“I’ve been wondering whether some of my child’s challenges might be related to prenatal alcohol exposure, even if we don’t know for sure. Can you help me understand what would be involved in finding out?”
  • 1. What would an FASD evaluation involve for my child?
  • 2. Is there a specialist or clinic in our area that does FASD evaluations?
  • 3. What records or testing would be helpful to gather beforehand?
  • 4. Would a referral to a developmental pediatrician or neuropsychologist be appropriate?
  • 5. How long does the evaluation process typically take?
  • 6. What can an FASD evaluation tell us, and what can it not tell us?
  • 7. How can we use the results to support my child at home and at school?

3. What Records to Bring to the Appointment

Having these available can help your doctor make a more informed referral:

  • □ School evaluations, IEP, or 504 plan (if applicable)
  • □ Prior psychological or neuropsychological testing
  • □ Therapy records (behavioral, speech, occupational, physical)
  • □ Medical and developmental history (growth, milestones, hospitalizations)
  • □ Caregiver observations: learning, behavior, attention, adaptive skills, daily functioning
  • □ Any available information about prenatal alcohol exposure, if known

4. Framing the Conversation

How you frame the conversation can shape how your child’s doctor responds. Some approaches that may help:

  • ✓ Focus on specific concerns: “My child is struggling with X, Y, and Z, and I’m trying to understand why.”
  • ✓ Be open about what you don’t know: “We don’t have confirmed information about prenatal exposure, but we’re trying to understand what might be going on.”
  • ✓ Ask about next steps, not a diagnosis: “What would help us find out whether an evaluation would be useful?”
  • ✓ Bring your observations: “Here’s what we’re seeing at home and at school.” Written notes can be very helpful.

5. What an FASD Evaluation Can and Cannot Answer

Can provide:

  • • Neurodevelopmental profile (strengths and challenges)
  • • Diagnostic clarification when criteria are met
  • • Recommendations for support at home, school, and in the community
  • • A framework for understanding your child’s behavior

Cannot provide:

  • • A guarantee of diagnosis
  • • Immediate access to services or funding
  • • A court-ready or DSS-ready report
  • • A quick fix or simple explanation for complex behaviors

6. After the Referral — What to Expect

  • 1. Wait time varies. FASD specialty clinics may have waiting lists of several months.
  • 2. Multiple visits may be needed. Evaluation often requires gathering information from multiple sources before a diagnostic conclusion can be reached.
  • 3. Keep gathering records. The more information available when the evaluation happens, the more complete it can be.
  • 4. Ask about support in the meantime. Your doctor may be able to recommend interim strategies, school supports, or community resources while you wait.
  • 5. Not an emergency service. FASD evaluation is a diagnostic process, not a crisis intervention. If your child is in crisis, seek immediate support through your doctor or local crisis services.

7. Important Boundaries

  • ⛔ This guide is educational. It does not diagnose FASD, guarantee access to evaluation, or promise a specific outcome.
  • ⛔ FASD evaluation should occur through appropriate clinical intake. This is not an emergency service or open referral channel.
  • ⛔ Any clinical evaluation should be conducted by qualified professionals. This guide does not replace clinical judgment.
  • ⛔ This material is not court-ready, DSS-ready, or adoption-agency-ready. It is designed for family education and PCP referral preparation only.
