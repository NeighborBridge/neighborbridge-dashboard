Work Protocol — Local Facilitator: Church-Network Caregiving Formation

Role label (internal only): Church Caring Network Helper / Local Relationship Facilitator (教会关系网络轻量协助者)

Critical constraint: This protocol is an internal operational working note. It is not a job description, employment agreement, independent contractor agreement, or offer of any kind. It does not authorize Hannah to act on Richard's behalf, represent any organization, or interact with institutions. Any paid role requires separate legal review by a North Carolina attorney before commencement.

STRUCTURE OVERVIEW

The protocol is organized into 4 work streams. Each work stream has a scope, a first-step action, guardrails, and a check-in trigger. Work streams are designed to be parallel or sequential depending on readiness.

Work Stream A: Relationship Map — Understanding the Church Community

Goal: Create a high-level, non-sensitive relationship map that helps Richard understand who in the church community is naturally positioned for friendship-based or practical support. This is not a directory or database — it is Richard's personal understanding tool.

Hannah's role: Guiding informant. She helps Richard understand relationship patterns, not specific individuals' private information.

  • Step A1 — Orientation conversation: Richard sits with Hannah (and optionally her parents) for a 30-minute conversation. Topics: church size, typical age distribution, families vs singles, who consistently shows up for fellowship meals or informal help, what types of help naturally happen in the church (meals, visits, rides, prayer). Richard leads; Hannah responds.
  • Step A2 — Broad categories, not names: Hannah helps Richard sketch categories: retired adults who are active, families with older teens who may help, young adults who attend regularly, members with healthcare background, members with practical skills (handyman, driving, cooking). Document categories only, not individual names unless Richard and Hannah agree a specific person is genuinely trustworthy and would welcome gentle contact.
  • Step A3 — Relationship strengths: Hannah helps Richard understand what each category might comfortably offer: e.g., families with teens may appreciate a service-oriented activity, members with healthcare background are likely already overwhelmed but may give advice, retired adults may welcome meaningful visiting opportunities.
  • Step A4 — Initial map: Richard sketches a simple one-page relationship map on paper or a non-sensitive digital document. Categories + approximate relationship strengths + gentle-contact readiness. No PHI. No private details. Hannah reviews for accuracy.

Guardrail: No individual names on the map without explicit permission. No private health or family information. The map is Richard's personal tool, not a database, spreadsheet, or product asset.

Check-in trigger: When Richard can describe the church community in 4-6 broad categories and identify 1-2 categories with natural helper positioning.

Work Stream B: Communication & Language — How to Ask Without Pressure

Goal: Develop natural, low-pressure language for gently inviting church members to consider simple, optional, non-clinical support — without creating obligation, guilt, or transactional framing.

Hannah's role: Cultural advisor. She helps Richard hear what a request sounds like inside her church culture.

  • Step B1 — Natural language audit: Richard reads draft language aloud to Hannah. She listens for: too formal, too transactional, too demanding, too vague, sounds like a business proposal, sounds like a job posting, sounds like a church program.
  • Step B2 — Church idiom adaptation: Hannah helps translate Richard's intended request into language that sounds like a natural church conversation. What words are safe? What words create pressure? How would the pastor normally invite help?
  • Step B3 — Pastor-family framing check: Hannah helps Richard understand what the pastor (her father) would find appropriate vs inappropriate. This is separate from asking the pastor for permission — it is understanding the church's communication culture.
  • Step B4 — Example language: Richard and Hannah co-create 2-3 sample gentle invitation phrases for different types of support. These are practice drafts, not messages to send yet. Examples: "I'm checking if anyone might naturally enjoy an occasional visit — no schedule, no pressure." / "We're just learning what kinds of help feel natural in our church."
  • Step B5 — Tone calibration: Hannah helps Richard calibrate for: warmth without obligation, clarity without formality, gratitude without indebtedness, invitation without recruitment language, time-limited rather than open-ended.

Guardrail: No language that implies: a business offer, a paid position, a formal volunteer role with shifts, a NeighborBridge program, a research study or interview, or any commitment. All language must be optional, low-pressure, relational.

Check-in trigger: When Richard and Hannah have 2-3 practice phrases that feel natural, and Richard can say each phrase without feeling like he is making a pitch, program pitch, or formal ask.

Work Stream C: Support-Type Mapping — What Help Looks Like in This Church

Goal: Develop a concrete but flexible understanding of what types of support the church community could naturally provide — and what types are unrealistic, uncomfortable, or inappropriate in this context.

Hannah's role: Reality-check partner. She helps Richard avoid assuming what support is feasible.

  • Step C1 — Natural support types: Hannah and Richard list what kinds of help she has observed in this church: meal trains, hospital visits, short-term family support after a death, prayer chains, childcare swapping, rides to church, weekly check-in calls.
  • Step C2 — Fit-for-Richard filter: For each type, assess: Is this natural for Richard's situation? Does the church have capacity for this type? Would the pastor family feel comfortable with this? Does Richard need this type of support? Does this overlap with Bayada responsibilities?
  • Step C3 — Gap identification: Where do Richard's actual needs sit relative to what the church naturally does? Meals may be natural. Regular respite visits may not be. Richard needs to see gaps clearly before deciding what to ask for.
  • Step C4 — Non-starters: Hannah helps identify what will never work in her church context: professional-style caregiving shifts, scheduled coverage that looks like a job, anything that feels like running a business through the church, anything that creates liability concern for the church.
  • Step C5 — Minimum viable ask: Hannah and Richard together identify the single smallest, most natural form of support to start with — ideally something that already happens informally that could be extended slightly.

Guardrail: No assumption that any support type is guaranteed. No commitment from anyone. No converting natural help into a schedule or roster. No creating a formal church program. No asking for clinical or medical support.

Check-in trigger: When Richard can list 3-4 support types that are natural in this church, 2-3 that might fit his situation, and 2-3 that are clearly non-starters.

Work Stream D: Gentle Pilot — Optional / Later (not automatic)

⚠ This work stream is optional and later. It should not be launched automatically after Work Streams A-C. Even a small pilot (meal support, weekly check-in call) begins to involve real church members and real relational expectations. That should happen only if all activation conditions are confirmed.

Goal: If activated, run a single, very small, time-limited pilot action that tests whether the church-network concept is realistic — without launching a program, making promises, or creating expectations. If this works, learn from it. If it doesn't, no loss.

Work Stream D activation conditions (all must be met):

  1. Phase 1 gentle conversation completed.
  2. Work Streams A-C completed and felt natural, low-pressure, comfortable.
  3. Hannah is comfortable with further involvement.
  4. Pastor-family comfort is confirmed.
  5. Richard feels the church culture can support a small ask without guilt, pressure, or misunderstanding.
  6. No PHI, payment, caregiving responsibility, or formal program language is involved.

Hannah's role (if activated): Pilot partner. She may help Richard understand whether the pilot is working, but should not be the point person or responsible person.

  • Step D1 — Select pilot action: From Work Stream C, pick the single smallest support action. Examples: "Check if one church member would naturally bring a meal once a week for 3 weeks." Or "Ask if one person would call Richard's mother for a 10-minute friendly check-in once a week for 2 weeks."
  • Step D2 — Gentle invitation: Using language from Work Stream B, Richard (not Hannah) extends a gentle, optional invitation to the selected person. No pressure. Explicitly time-limited. No obligation to continue.
  • Step D3 — Run pilot: The pilot runs for 1-2 weeks only. Richard observes: Was the interaction natural? Did the person feel comfortable? Did Richard's mother feel comfortable? Was there any misunderstanding about role or expectations?
  • Step D4 — Debrief with Hannah: Richard and Hannah discuss: What worked? What felt awkward? What would need to change for a similar ask? Was the person's comfort maintained? Did Richard inadvertently create pressure?
  • Step D5 — Document learning: Richard writes a 3-5 sentence internal learning note. What did this pilot teach about church-network feasibility for Richard's situation? What should not be repeated? What was surprisingly positive?
  • Step D6 — Pause and assess: After the pilot, the protocol pauses. Do not expand. Do not recruit more people. Do not create a roster or schedule. Reflect: Is this worth pursuing in any form? If yes, what must change structurally?

Guardrail: Maximum 2 weeks. Maximum 1-2 people. No schedule or recurring expectation. No payment in pilot phase (unless attorney reviewed). No public mention. No NeighborBridge identifier. No church program name. No assumption that this becomes a regular thing.

Decision required before activation: Richard must make a separate, explicit go/no-go decision before Work Stream D begins. Do not default to launching after A-C. If go: what next steps require Clara review? What requires attorney review?

  • No institutional representation: Hannah must not represent herself as Richard's assistant, NeighborBridge staff, Yale/Duke affiliate, Contentnea representative, or NCFASD spokesperson.
  • No research framing: This is not a research study. No data collection, surveys, interviews with recording, IRB-equivalent process, or systematic knowledge extraction. Hannah is a local facilitator, not a research assistant.
  • No public mention: This protocol and all work streams are internal only. Nothing from this work may appear on NeighborBridge public website, Trillium materials, grant applications, academic manuscripts (unless sufficiently abstracted and non-identifying), or church materials.
  • No product pilot: This is not a pilot of a NeighborBridge product, Church Caring Network product, Covenant Care program, or any commercial offering. This is Richard's personal exploration of whether local church support is realistic.

Small churches may have trust, relational continuity, and informal support capacity — but informal support can also create pressure, ambiguity, privacy risk, role confusion, and liability concerns. The design must preserve warmth while protecting boundaries.
